Abstract
Distillation of a language model intended to trans-
fer benign behavior to a student model may also
transfer undesirable characteristics, if they are
present in the teacher model, a phenomenon
known as subliminal learning. While qualitative
evidence supports the existence of this effect, its
magnitude has not been systematically charac-
terized. This study quantifies subliminal behav-
ioral transfer ratios by steering two teacher mod-
els (Llama-2-7B-Chat and Qwen2.5-7B-Instruct)
at varying steering strengths and distilling stu-
dent models using only benign data. Evalua-
tion on 100 JailbreakBench prompts with GPT-
4.1, serving as the evaluator, indicates that trans-
fer is robust but exhibits distinct scaling behav-
iors. Llama-2 demonstrates a sharp threshold
(τ= 0.25,0.32beyondα=−0.15 ), whereas
Qwen2.5 displays continuous and higher levels of
transfer (τup to0.61).
1. Introduction
Knowledge distillation (Gou et al., 2021; Mansourian et al.,
2025) from large teacher models has become a standard
technique for model compression, especially in resource-
constrained deployment scenarios. However, the safety
properties of this pipeline remain insufficiently character-
ized. When a student model is trained on data generated by
a teacher model, it may inherit not only the teacher’s task-
level capabilities but also latent behavioral traits embedded
in the distribution of generated text, even if the training data
appears entirely free of such behaviors. Viewed through the
lens of epistemic intelligence, subliminal transfer represents
a profound epistemic failure: the student model acquires

Recent findings offer a concrete mechanistic basis for this
concern. Research in mechanistic interpretability has re-
vealed that refusal in chat-aligned language models is me-
diated by a dominant linear direction in the residual stream
(Arditi et al., 2024), which means that safety alignment
can be surgically degraded by subtracting a single vector.
Complementary work has shown that harmful or deceptive
outputs leave detectable signatures in model activations, mo-
tivating monitoring-based defenses (Chaudhary & Barez,
2025). Separately, Cloud et al. (2025) demonstrated that
when a student is fine-tuned on data generated by a be-
haviorally modified teacher, the teacher’s latent traits are
transferred even when the training data is not related to the
behavior, a phenomenon termedsubliminal learning.
Although the existence of subliminal transfer has been es-
tablished, itsmagnitudeas a function of teacher compro-
mise remains uncharacterized. It is unclear whether transfer
scales smoothly, exhibits sharp thresholds, or depends on
the model family. Without quantitative analysis, assessing

2. Background
Chat-aligned language models.Modern language mod-
els deployed for instruction following undergo a post-
training alignment stage, such as reinforcement learning
from human feedback (RLHF) (Ouyang et al., 2022) or di-
rect preference optimization (Rafailov et al., 2023), which
trains the model to refuse harmful requests. The resulting
behavior, commonly termedrefusal, is a core component of
deployed safety.
Refusal directions and activation steering.Recent work
in mechanistic interpretability has shown that refusal is
encoded as a linear direction in the model’s residual stream
(Arditi et al., 2024). Concretely, given hidden states hℓ ∈
Rd at layer ℓ, one can extract a direction vector vℓ from
the difference in mean activations between harmful and
harmless prompts. Modifying the hidden state as given in
Equation 1
˜hℓ =h ℓ +α·v ℓ (1)
steers the model’s behavior: negative α suppresses refusal
while positive α amplifies it (Turner et al., 2023; Zou et al.,
2023). Related work has shown that such directional inter-
ventions generalize across behavioral axes, including syco-
phancy reduction via off-the-shelf persona vectors (Kelkar
et al., 2026) and prevention of reasoning-trace leakage in
chain-of-thought via activation steering (Batra et al., 2025).
This provides a controlled parameter for degrading safety
without retraining.
Knowledge distillation.Knowledge distillation (Hinton
et al., 2015) trains a student model on data generated by
the teacher (imitation-based SFT), or more classically, by
minimizing the KL divergence between their logit distri-
butions. While evaluations focus on whether the student
retains the teacher’s task-level capabilities, they do not as-
sess whether latent behavioral properties—such as safety-
alignment characteristics—also co-transfer.
Subliminal learning.Cloud et al. (2025) demonstrated
that fine-tuning a student on data generated by a behav-
iorally modified teacher transmits the teacher’s latent traits,
even when the training data is unrelated to the behavior, a
phenomenon they termsubliminal learning. Their work
establishes the existence of this transfer but does not quan-
tify how its magnitude scales with the degree of teacher
compromise. Our work addresses precisely this gap.
3. Methodology
Our pipeline has different stages (Figure 2): refusal direction
extraction, teacher steering, data generation, and student
distillation. All experiments use Llama-2-7B-Chat (Touvron
et al., 2023) (32 layers,d= 4096 ) and Qwen2.5-7B-Instruct
(Yang et al., 2025) (28 layers,d= 3584).

(2024), we construct a contrastive dataset of 148 harm-
ful and 165 harmless prompts and record the hidden state
hℓ,−1 ∈R d at the final token position for every layer ℓ. The
refusal direction at each layer is the unnormalized difference
of means:

separability between refusal and non-refusal activations. For
Qwen2.5-7B-Instruct, we follow the same procedure and
selectv 18.

refusal. We use five steering coefficients for distillation:
α∈ {−0.05,−0.10,−0.15,−0.20,−0.25}.

4. Experiments and Results

Teacher safety under steering.Both teachers degrade
nonlinearly with steering strength. For Llama-2, moderate
coefficients (α=−0.05 to −0.10) produce gradual degra-

45%), reaching 94% at α=−0.20 with degenerate outputs
emerging (1% at -0.20, 19% at -0.25). Qwen2.5 degrades
more smoothly (23% → 59% → 83% → 92%) with zero

Subliminal transfer .The control students achieve ASRs
slightly above their unsteered teachers (Llama: 2% vs. 1%;
Qwen: 6% vs. 4%). This minor baseline drift during QLoRA
fine-tuning provides a conservative downward pressure on
our τ estimates. The results reveal two distinct regimes
(Figure 1). In the sub-threshold regime ( α=−0.05 to
−0.15), teacher ASR rises to as high as 45%, yet student
ASR remains between 1% and 5%, with τ hovering near
zero (note that the negative τ=−0.08 at α=−0.10 and
the erratic estimates at α=−0.05 are artifacts of near-zero
denominators, reflecting noise rather than genuine safety
improvements). A sharp transition occurs at α=−0.20 ,
where student ASR rises to 25% (τ= 0.25 ); at α=−0.25 ,
it reaches 33% ( τ= 0.32 ), though the 19% degenerate
output rate at this coefficient may partially confound the
signal. Wilson score 95% confidence intervals confirm the
threshold: student ASR at α=−0.20 (CI: [17.5, 34.3])
does not overlap with the control (CI: [0.6, 7.0]).

Qwen2.5 shows a qualitatively different pattern. Its con-
trol student reaches 6% ASR (teacher: 4%), and transfer
rises continuously from α=−0.10 onward: τ= 0.32 at
α=−0.10 , 0.35 at α=−0.15 , 0.44 at α=−0.20 , and
0.61 at α=−0.25 . Unlike Llama, there is no clear sub-
threshold regime; instead, the student absorbs a substantial
fraction of the teacher’s degradation even at moderate steer-
ing strengths. At α=−0.25 , the student reaches 60% ASR
(95% CI: [49.7, 69.4], which strictly does not overlap with
the control CI of [2.8, 12.5]). Appendix B illustrates this
with a qualitative example. Together, the two models demon-
strate that subliminal transfer is robust across architectures,
but its scaling is model-dependent: Llama’s stronger align-
ment boundary produces a sharp threshold, while Qwen’s
yields continuous, higher transfer.
5. Analysis
Cross-model transfer dynamics.Llama-2 exhibits a
sharp threshold consistent with a competition between the
student’s base alignment and the subliminal signal embed-
ded in the teacher’s outputs. Qwen2.5 shows no such thresh-
old; transfer scales continuously, reaching τ= 0.61 at
α=−0.25 . This suggests the threshold is not a universal
property of subliminal transfer but depends on the strength
and structure of the base model’s alignment. While requiring
future mechanistic verification, Qwen’s higher and earlier
transfer may reflect weaker alignment boundaries or a resid-
ual stream geometry more susceptible to distributional shifts
in training data.
Crucially, Qwen2.5’s continuous scaling rules out the triv-
ial hypothesis that Llama-2’s threshold simply stems from
insufficient unsafe data at mild steering ( α=−0.05 ,
α=−0.10 ). Because Qwen2.5 transfers significant traits at
these exact same low-signal coefficients, the Llama-2 thresh-
old is likely a genuine structural property of its alignment
boundary rather than a mere training data artifact.
Coherence degradation at extreme steering.At α=
−0.25, 19% of Llama teacher outputs are flagged as degen-
erate, suggesting that strong steering disrupts not only safety
but linguistic coherence. The cleaner operating point is at
α=−0.20(1% degenerate,τ= 0.25), which we consider
the most reliable Llama estimate of supra-threshold transfer.
Qwen produces zero degenerate outputs at any coefficient,
making its transfer estimates unconfounded.
6. Conclusion
This study introduced a methodology for measuring sub-
liminal behavioral transfer ratios and applied it to two
model families across five levels of teacher compromise.
Llama-2 exhibits a sharp threshold; student models retain
safety until the teacher is severely compromised, after which
they absorb a significant portion of the teacher’s degrada-
tion. Specifically, Llama students absorb 25–32% of the
degradation, while Qwen transfer increases continuously
to τ= 0.61 . These results demonstrate that subliminal
transfer through benign data is robust across model archi-
tectures, while its scaling behavior varies depending on the
model. This underscores the need for behavioral auditing of
distillation pipelines that extends beyond content-level data
inspection. Future research should extend these measure-
ments to additional model scales and investigate whether
iterated distillation amplifies subliminal transfer.
Limitations
Both experiments use 7B-parameter models; transfer ratios
and scaling patterns may differ at other scales or with dif-
ferent alignment procedures. Because prior work (Cloud
et al., 2025) suggests subliminal learning may fail across
fundamentally different base architectures, our findings
specifically characterize the threat model of intra-family
distillation (e.g., Llama-to-Llama). We evaluate on 100
JailbreakBench prompts; the sample size introduces sam-
pling uncertainty in τ estimates. Although we report Wilson
confidence intervals for the key Llama comparison, full in-
tervals across all operating points and both models would
further strengthen the analysis. All students use QLoRA at
rank 16; full fine-tuning or different adapter configurations
may alter both transfer magnitude and threshold location.
Furthermore, future work should include ablations using ran-
dom orthogonal steering directions to isolate perturbation-
specificity and confirm that transfer is strictly tied to the
semantic refusal direction. Finally, we measure a single
round of distillation. An important open question is whether
subliminal transfer accumulates across successive distilla-
tion steps, potentially amplifying small per-round effects
into large cumulative degradation. More broadly, different
post-training procedures appear to leave distinct mechanistic
footprints (Nunez et al., 2026), suggesting that distillation-
mediated subliminal transfer may interact with the choice
of subsequent fine-tuning objective on the student. More
broadly, different post-training procedures appear to leave
distinct mechanistic footprints (Nunez et al., 2026), sug-
gesting that distillation-mediated subliminal transfer may
interact with the choice of subsequent fine-tuning objective
on the student. Finally, relying on GPT-4.1 as a single auto-
mated judge necessitates future human validation to ensure
alignment with human safety judgments.
Broader Impact
This work investigates a vulnerability in language model
distillation pipelines: safety degradation can propagate sub-
liminally through benign training data. We acknowledge

that the described methodology of using activation steer-

However, activation steering is already well-documented in
the literature (Arditi et al., 2024; Turner et al., 2023), and
our contribution is the measurement of transfer rather than

magnitude of subliminal transfer is a necessary step toward

the transfer ratio. We encourage practitioners who distill
from external teachers (especially those sharing a base ar-

their workflows, even when training data appears benign.
Future defensive research might explore monitoring teacher
activation trajectories during data generation or applying la-
